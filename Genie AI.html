Explain pascal's triangle theorem

Pascal's Triangle is a triangular array of numbers where each number is the sum of the two numbers directly above it. Here's a quick rundown:
How it works:
The triangle starts with a 1 at the top.
Each row has one more number than the previous row.
The numbers on the ends are always 1.
The numbers in between are the sum of the two numbers directly above.
Example:
Code
1
1 1
1 2 1
1 3 3 1
1 4 6 4 1
Cool properties:
Binomial coefficients: Pascal's Triangle shows the coefficients of binomial expansions (a+b)^n.
Symmetry: The triangle is symmetrical.
Combinations: Each number represents the number of combinations of items.
Real-world applications:
Probability: Calculating odds and probabilities.
Algebra: Expanding binomials and polynomials.
Combinatorics: Counting combinations and permutations.
